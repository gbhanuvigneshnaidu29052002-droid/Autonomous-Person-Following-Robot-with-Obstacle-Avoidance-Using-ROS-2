VI. TRACKING MODULE: BYTETRACK AND PERSON RE-IDENTIFICATION
A. Introduction to the Tracking Module
While the perception module is responsible for detecting all visible persons in each camera frame, detection alone is insufficient for autonomous person-following. Since YOLO processes every frame independently, it does not maintain the identity of detected individuals over time. Consequently, the robot cannot determine whether a person detected in the current frame is the same individual detected in previous frames.

To overcome this limitation, the proposed system integrates the ByteTrack multi-object tracking algorithm. ByteTrack associates human detections across consecutive frames and assigns a unique tracking identity (Track ID) to each detected person. These persistent identities enable the robot to continuously follow a specific individual while ignoring other people in the environment.

In addition, Person Re-Identification (Re-ID) is incorporated to improve robustness under temporary target occlusions. When the designated person becomes hidden behind an obstacle or another individual, the Re-ID module compares the visual appearance of newly detected persons with the stored appearance of the original target. This allows the robot to reacquire the correct person after occlusion without switching to another individual.

The combination of ByteTrack and Person Re-Identification significantly improves tracking stability and enables reliable long-term autonomous person-following in crowded indoor environments.

B. ByteTrack Multi-Object Tracking
ByteTrack is a modern tracking-by-detection algorithm that maintains object identities by associating detections across consecutive image frames. Unlike traditional tracking algorithms that discard low-confidence detections, ByteTrack utilizes both high-confidence and low-confidence detections during data association. This approach improves tracking robustness when detections are partially occluded or have temporarily reduced confidence.

The tracking process consists of the following stages:

Receive person detections from the YOLO detection module.

Predict the current position of previously tracked persons.

Match new detections with existing tracks based on motion consistency and confidence.

Update the corresponding Track IDs.

Create new Track IDs for previously unseen individuals.

Remove inactive tracks after a predefined timeout period.

As a result, every detected person receives a unique identifier that remains consistent across multiple frames, allowing the navigation controller to follow a specific individual continuously.

C. Tracking ID Assignment
Each detected individual is assigned a unique numerical identity known as the Track ID. This identity remains unchanged as long as the person remains visible or can be successfully associated with previous observations.

For example, consider an indoor environment containing three individuals.

The robot selects one Track ID as the designated target and continuously follows that individual. Even if multiple people are present within the camera view, the robot ignores all other Track IDs.

This mechanism prevents accidental switching between different individuals during robot navigation.

D. Person Re-Identification (Re-ID)
Although ByteTrack provides robust tracking, temporary target loss may occur due to environmental occlusions such as shelves, walls, furniture, or moving pedestrians. When visual contact is interrupted for several frames, the tracking algorithm may terminate the existing track.

To overcome this problem, the proposed system integrates a Person Re-Identification (Re-ID) module.

Person Re-ID compares the visual appearance of newly detected individuals with previously stored feature representations of the designated target. Instead of relying only on position information, Re-ID extracts discriminative visual features including:

Clothing color

Body shape

Texture patterns

Deep feature embeddings

When the target reappears after occlusion, these appearance descriptors are compared with stored target features. If the similarity exceeds a predefined threshold, the robot restores the original Track ID and resumes person-following automatically.

This significantly improves system reliability, particularly in crowded indoor environments where multiple individuals may have similar trajectories.

E. Occlusion Recovery Strategy
Occlusion handling is an essential requirement for autonomous person-following robots operating in realistic indoor environments.

The proposed recovery strategy consists of four sequential stages:

Stage 1 – Target Visible
The target is continuously detected by YOLO and tracked by ByteTrack. The robot follows the target normally while maintaining the desired safety distance.

Stage 2 – Temporary Occlusion
The target becomes hidden behind an obstacle or another person. During this period, ByteTrack attempts to maintain the track using motion prediction.

Stage 3 – Target Reappearance
Once the target becomes visible again, the Person Re-Identification module compares appearance features with the stored target representation.

Stage 4 – Target Recovery
If a successful match is obtained, the original Track ID is restored and the robot resumes normal person-following behavior without user intervention.

H. Performance Evaluation
The tracking module was evaluated during both Gazebo simulation and real-world experiments using the TurtleBot3 Waffle Pi platform. Performance was assessed under various conditions, including multiple pedestrians, temporary target occlusions, and varying lighting conditions.

The results demonstrated that ByteTrack successfully maintained consistent target identities throughout most navigation scenarios. Temporary occlusions caused only brief interruptions, and the Person Re-Identification module effectively restored the original target identity after reappearance. Minor tracking degradation was observed during prolonged occlusions and extremely crowded scenes; however, overall tracking stability remained satisfactory for autonomous indoor navigation.

The integration of ByteTrack and Person Re-Identification significantly improved the reliability of the person-following system compared with approaches relying solely on object detection.

IX. REAL-WORLD DEPLOYMENT AND EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
A. Hardware Deployment
After successful validation in the Gazebo simulation environment, the complete autonomous person-following system was deployed on a TurtleBot3 Waffle Pi mobile robot. The physical robot served as the experimental platform for evaluating the performance of the perception, tracking, navigation, and obstacle avoidance modules under real-world indoor conditions.

The hardware configuration consisted of an Intel RealSense D435i RGB camera mounted at the front of the robot for visual perception and an RPLidar A1M8 laser scanner positioned above the robot chassis to provide a complete 360° view of the surrounding environment. The TurtleBot3 platform was equipped with a Raspberry Pi 4 and an OpenCR motor controller, enabling differential drive motion and communication with ROS 2.

Due to the computational requirements of deep-learning inference, the perception modules (YOLO and ByteTrack) were executed on an external laptop running Ubuntu 24.04 and ROS 2 Humble Hawksbill. The laptop communicated with the TurtleBot3 through the ROS 2 network, while the robot executed motion commands received through the /cmd_vel topic. This distributed architecture reduced the computational load on the onboard processor while maintaining real-time performance.

B. System Integration
The complete autonomous system was developed as a collection of independent ROS 2 nodes. Each node performed a specific function while exchanging information through ROS topics.

The system integration process involved the following sequence:

The RealSense camera continuously captured RGB images.

The YOLO detection node identified all visible persons.

ByteTrack assigned tracking identities.

Person Re-Identification maintained the identity of the designated target.

The LiDAR node continuously monitored surrounding obstacles.

The navigation controller combined tracking and obstacle information.

Motion commands were transmitted to the TurtleBot3 through the /cmd_vel topic.

The robot executed the commands and updated its position.

This modular architecture allowed each subsystem to operate independently while maintaining synchronized communication.

C. Experimental Procedure
The proposed system was evaluated under several indoor experimental scenarios designed to assess its performance in realistic operating conditions.

Experiment 1: Continuous Person Following
A designated user walked along a predefined path containing straight segments and gentle turns. The robot continuously detected and followed the target while maintaining a safe distance. The experiment verified the ability of the perception and tracking modules to maintain stable target identification.

Experiment 2: Obstacle Avoidance
Static obstacles such as tables, chairs, and boxes were positioned along the robot’s path. Whenever an obstacle entered the predefined safety zone, the navigation controller temporarily modified the robot's trajectory before returning to the original following path.

Experiment 3: Target Occlusion
The target intentionally moved behind an obstacle, temporarily disappearing from the camera view. During this period, the ByteTrack algorithm maintained track prediction while the Person Re-Identification module searched for the reappearing target. Once the target became visible again, the robot successfully resumed person-following without user intervention.

Experiment 4: Multiple People
Several individuals walked within the camera field of view. The robot consistently followed only the selected target, demonstrating the effectiveness of ByteTrack and Re-ID in preventing identity switching.

D. Experimental Results
The proposed system demonstrated reliable performance during all experimental scenarios. The perception module successfully detected the designated person under normal indoor lighting conditions, while ByteTrack maintained consistent target identities throughout most experiments. Temporary target occlusions were effectively handled by the Person Re-Identification module, enabling the robot to reacquire the original target after visual interruption.

The LiDAR-based obstacle avoidance module successfully detected nearby obstacles and generated smooth avoidance maneuvers without significant interruption to person-following behavior. During testing, the robot maintained stable motion and successfully navigated around shelves, tables, and walls commonly encountered in indoor environments.

E. Challenges Encountered
Although the proposed system performed successfully, several practical challenges were encountered during implementation and testing.

Lighting Variations
Camera-based person detection was affected by significant changes in illumination. Poor lighting occasionally reduced the confidence of the YOLO detector, particularly when the target moved into darker areas.

Temporary Occlusions
When the target remained hidden for an extended period, the tracking module occasionally required additional time to correctly restore the target identity after reappearance.

Limited Computational Resources
Running deep-learning algorithms on embedded hardware remains computationally demanding. To maintain real-time operation, perception tasks were executed on an external laptop while robot control remained onboard.

Network Communication
Since perception and navigation were distributed across two computing devices, stable wireless communication between the laptop and TurtleBot3 was essential. Temporary communication delays occasionally introduced small navigation latency.

Sensor Calibration
Accurate alignment between the RGB camera and LiDAR was necessary to ensure reliable obstacle avoidance. Sensor calibration was therefore an important step during system setup.

F. Discussion
The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed ROS 2-based autonomous person-following system successfully integrates modern computer vision and robotic navigation techniques into a unified framework. The combination of YOLO, ByteTrack, Person Re-Identification, and LiDAR-based obstacle avoidance enabled the robot to operate reliably in dynamic indoor environments while maintaining safe interaction with humans.

Compared with conventional person-following systems that rely solely on camera-based tracking, the proposed architecture provides greater robustness against temporary target loss and environmental obstacles. The modular ROS 2 implementation also simplifies future expansion, allowing additional perception or navigation algorithms to be incorporated with minimal software modification.

Although some limitations remain, particularly regarding computational requirements and prolonged target occlusions, the overall experimental evaluation confirms that the proposed system provides a practical and scalable solution for indoor autonomous service robotics.

X. CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS
A. Challenges Encountered
Although the proposed autonomous person-following robot demonstrated reliable performance during both simulation and real-world experiments, several practical challenges were encountered throughout the development and deployment phases. These challenges primarily involved perception reliability, computational constraints, sensor integration, and environmental uncertainties.

1. Lighting Variations
The YOLO-based perception module relies on RGB images captured by the Intel RealSense D435i camera. Significant changes in illumination, shadows, and reflections occasionally reduced detection confidence, particularly in poorly lit areas. Although YOLO maintained acceptable detection performance, lighting variations affected the consistency of person detection.

2. Temporary Target Occlusions
During navigation, the target person occasionally became hidden behind shelves, walls, or other pedestrians. While ByteTrack and Person Re-Identification successfully recovered the target in most situations, prolonged occlusions sometimes increased recovery time and introduced short interruptions in the following behavior.

3. Limited Onboard Computing Resources
Deep learning algorithms require considerable computational resources for real-time inference. Since the Raspberry Pi onboard the TurtleBot3 has limited processing capability, the perception modules were executed on an external Ubuntu workstation. Although this architecture enabled smooth operation, it introduced network dependency between the robot and the external computer.

4. Network Communication
Reliable communication between the laptop and TurtleBot3 was essential for real-time operation. Temporary wireless communication delays occasionally introduced minor latency in transmitting velocity commands, particularly when the network experienced interference.

5. Sensor Calibration
Accurate calibration between the Intel RealSense camera and the RPLidar sensor was necessary to ensure consistent perception and obstacle avoidance. Misalignment between sensors could reduce navigation accuracy, requiring careful installation and calibration before experimentation.

B. System Limitations
Although the proposed system successfully achieved its objectives, several limitations remain.

The current implementation follows only a single designated person and does not support simultaneous tracking of multiple target users.

The system has been evaluated primarily in indoor environments and has not been optimized for outdoor operation, where varying weather conditions and changing illumination can significantly affect perception.

Navigation relies mainly on local obstacle avoidance and does not incorporate global path planning or semantic mapping of large environments.

The implementation depends on an external computer for deep learning inference, limiting portability for fully standalone deployment.

Very dense crowds and prolonged complete occlusions may reduce tracking reliability.

These limitations provide opportunities for future research and system enhancement.

XI. FUTURE WORK
The proposed autonomous person-following robot establishes a strong foundation for future developments in intelligent service robotics. Several improvements can further enhance system capability, robustness, and practical deployment.

A. Multi-Person Tracking and Intelligent Target Selection
Future versions of the system can support simultaneous tracking of multiple individuals and implement intelligent target selection strategies. User identification based on face recognition, voice commands, or wearable devices can enable the robot to automatically distinguish its owner from surrounding people.

B. Improved Person Re-Identification
Although the current Person Re-Identification module performs satisfactorily, more advanced deep-learning models such as OSNet or FastReID can improve target recovery after long-duration occlusions and in crowded environments. GPU acceleration would further enhance inference speed and overall tracking performance.

C. Integration with ROS 2 Navigation Stack (Nav2)
Future work can integrate the complete ROS 2 Navigation Stack (Nav2) to enable advanced path planning, global and local costmaps, dynamic obstacle avoidance, and autonomous navigation over larger indoor environments.

D. Semantic Mapping and SLAM
The current implementation focuses on person following and obstacle avoidance without constructing a map of the environment. Integrating Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) techniques would allow the robot to build and maintain detailed maps while navigating. Semantic mapping could further classify objects such as doors, shelves, and furniture, enabling more intelligent navigation.

E. Gesture and Voice Recognition
Adding gesture recognition and speech processing would significantly improve human–robot interaction. The robot could respond to hand gestures or voice commands for selecting a target, stopping, changing speed, or navigating to predefined locations.

F. Edge AI Deployment
Future versions can employ embedded AI platforms such as NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano or Jetson Xavier NX to execute perception algorithms directly onboard the robot. This would eliminate dependence on an external computer and improve system portability.

G. Outdoor Navigation
The proposed system can be extended to outdoor environments by integrating GPS, IMU, stereo vision, and weather-robust perception algorithms. Such improvements would enable autonomous navigation in parks, campuses, and public spaces.

XII. CONCLUSION
This paper presented the design, implementation, and evaluation of an autonomous person-following robot with obstacle avoidance developed using the Robot Operating System 2 (ROS 2). The proposed system integrates deep-learning-based person detection using YOLO, multi-object tracking through ByteTrack, Person Re-Identification for target recovery, and LiDAR-based obstacle avoidance into a unified modular robotic architecture.

The system was initially validated in a Gazebo supermarket simulation environment, where perception, tracking, navigation, and obstacle avoidance algorithms were thoroughly tested under controlled conditions. Following successful simulation, the software was deployed on a TurtleBot3 Waffle Pi equipped with an Intel RealSense D435i RGB camera and an RPLidar A1M8 laser scanner. Experimental evaluation demonstrated reliable person detection, stable target tracking, effective obstacle avoidance, and successful recovery from temporary target occlusions.

The modular ROS 2 architecture proved highly effective for integrating multiple perception and navigation components while maintaining flexibility for future system expansion. The use of YOLO enabled accurate real-time person detection, ByteTrack provided stable target identities, and the Person Re-Identification module improved robustness during temporary visual interruptions. The LiDAR-based obstacle avoidance system ensured safe navigation around static and dynamic obstacles without significantly affecting person-following performance.

Overall, the proposed system demonstrates that combining modern computer vision, intelligent tracking algorithms, and ROS 2-based robotic control can produce a practical solution for autonomous indoor service robots. The developed framework has significant potential for applications in healthcare assistance, warehouse logistics, retail automation, elderly care, and smart public facilities. Future enhancements involving semantic mapping, advanced navigation, and multimodal human–robot interaction will further improve system intelligence and real-world applicability.
