SPEAKER NOTES:
1.7.2013 “This slide shows the overall ROS 2 system architecture. The RGB camera and 360-degree LiDAR send visual and distance information to the perception node. The perception node detects and tracks the selected person, then publishes the target information through the /tracked_target topic. This includes the target’s distance, angle, and tracking status—visible, occluded, or lost. The behavior tree node receives this target data, together with the LiDAR scan and robot odometry, and sends movement commands through /cmd_vel to the TurtleBot3 Waffle. The manager node controls the startup sequence: it waits for the system to initialize, sends the TRAIN command, waits until the target is visible, and then sends START. Finally, the dashboard monitors the camera, LiDAR, target status, robot state, odometry and logs, and also provides an emergency stop. So, in simple terms, perception determines who and where the target is, behavior decides what the robot should do, and the manager controls when the system begins.” ‹#›

 PROJECT OVERVIEW 03 A ROS 2 PERSON-FOLLOWING SYSTEM THA t  IDENTIFIES ONE PERSON, FOLLOWS SAFELY, AVOIDS OBSTACLES, AND RECOVERS THE TARGET AFTER OCCLUSION. TARGET PERCEPTION YOLO + BYTETRACK + RE-ID SAFE FOLLOWING CAMERA + 360° LIDAR RECOVERY BEHAVIOR REROUTE, SEARCH & REACQUIRE VALIDATION GAZEBO + REAL ROBOT

SPEAKER NOTES:
1.7.2013 “This slide explains the internal pipeline of the perception node. First, the RGB camera continuously sends images to the system. YOLO processes every image and detects all people visible in the scene. However, YOLO only detects the class ‘person’; it does not remember which person is our selected target. Therefore, we use ByteTrack. ByteTrack associates detections between consecutive video frames and assigns tracking IDs. This allows the system to follow the movement of each detected person over time. After that, we perform a 40-frame enrollment phase. During this phase, the selected person stands near the centre of the camera, and the system collects appearance information from multiple frames. Using 40 frames is more reliable than using only one image because the person may move, turn, or appear differently between frames. The stored appearance information is then used for re-identification, or Re-ID. Re-ID helps the system recognise the same selected person after an occlusion or after ByteTrack changes the person’s tracking ID. This prevents the robot from accidentally switching to another person. The target’s angle is estimated from their horizontal position in the camera image. If the person appears on the left, the robot needs to turn left; if they appear on the right, it turns right. The distance is mainly estimated using the LiDAR measurement in the direction of the detected person. Finally, the perception node publishes /tracked_target, containing target visibility, distance, angle and tracking information. It also publishes /yolo/debug_image, which is the camera image with bounding boxes and labels, allowing us to visually verify that detection and tracking are working correctly.” “The key difference is that YOLO detects people, ByteTrack follows detections between frames, and Re-ID confirms that the detected person is the same person we enrolled.” ‹#›

SPEAKER NOTES:
1.7.2013 "The Behavior node translates perception into physical action. We designed a state machine consisting of WAIT, FOLLOW, AVOID, REROUTE, SEARCH, and ACQUIRE states. When following, the robot uses a Proportional controller to smoothly adjust its linear and angular velocity to maintain distance. When an obstacle is detected, the robot smoothly transitions to the AVOID state. Here, we utilize a potential-field method that blends 70 percent obstacle repulsion with 30 percent target attraction, allowing the robot to steer around the blockage while maintaining its general heading toward the owner." ‹#›

MONITORING DASHBOARD 09

SPEAKER NOTES:
1.7.2013 "Deploying this system introduced several engineering challenges. Dynamic obstacles and occlusions in cluttered environments frequently tested our tracking logic. Furthermore, lighting variations heavily impacted the camera-based detection and our HSV color histograms. Hardware limitations were also a major factor; without a GPU, we had to heavily optimize our deep learning models for CPU inference. Finally, we had to overcome hardware-specific hurdles, such as QoS mismatches dropping our LiDAR data and the aforementioned motor brownouts during sharp maneuvers." ‹#›
